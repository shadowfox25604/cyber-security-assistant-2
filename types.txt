Different Parts or Aspects of Cyber Security
===========================================

1) Network Security
Brief:
Network security protects the pathways through which devices communicate, such as office Wi-Fi, internet routers, company switches, and private business networks. In simple words, it works like traffic police and gatekeepers for digital data, deciding which connection is trusted and which should be blocked. It is used anywhere multiple devices share information, including schools, hospitals, banks, factories, offices, and cloud-connected branches. Common protections include firewalls, VPNs, network segmentation, and monitoring tools. Together, these controls help prevent unauthorized access, reduce service outages, and keep daily digital operations safe and stable.

- What is the difference between a firewall and an IDS/IPS?
- How does network segmentation reduce security risk?
- What are common signs of a DDoS attack?

"You are a network security assistant. Explain concepts clearly, suggest practical defensive controls, and include step-by-step checks for firewalls, IDS/IPS, segmentation, and traffic monitoring."


2) Application Security
Brief:
Application security is the process of making software safe at every stage, from planning and coding to testing and live use. If an app is a building, application security checks every door, lock, and entry point so only the right users can get in and use features safely. It is used in websites, mobile apps, APIs, online banking systems, shopping platforms, hospital portals, and internal company tools. Typical practices include secure coding, regular vulnerability testing, patching, and runtime protection. This helps protect user data, payments, and business operations from misuse, fraud, and cyber attacks.

- What is secure code review and why is it important?

3) Endpoint Security
Brief:
Endpoint security protects every individual device connected to an organization, such as laptops, desktops, servers, tablets, and phones. In simple terms, each device is a possible entry door for attackers, and endpoint security adds locks, alarms, and monitoring to that door. It is used in offices, remote work environments, schools, hospitals, and companies with bring-your-own-device policies. Common controls include antivirus, EDR tools, patch updates, encryption, and device restrictions. These protections help stop malware, ransomware, and credential theft early, and allow security teams to quickly isolate compromised machines before the threat spreads.

- How do I harden Windows endpoints?

4) Cloud Security
Brief:
Cloud security protects applications, databases, files, and services that run on cloud platforms rather than only on local computers. You can think of cloud services as rented digital spaces, and cloud security makes sure those spaces have correct locks, access rules, and monitoring. It is used by startups, enterprises, schools, hospitals, and government teams for hosting websites, storing files, running backups, and powering modern apps. Main controls include identity permissions, encryption, secure configurations, network filtering, and activity logging. Good cloud security prevents accidental data exposure, unauthorized access, and downtime while supporting safe business growth.

5) Identity and Access Management (IAM)
Brief:
Identity and Access Management (IAM) controls digital identity and access in a simple way: verify who a user is, decide what they can use, and limit how long that access lasts. It works like ID cards and permission badges in a secure office building, but for software and data. IAM is used in email systems, HR tools, cloud dashboards, developer platforms, and customer portals. It includes passwords, MFA, single sign-on, and role-based access rules. Strong IAM prevents unauthorized access to sensitive information and makes it easier to safely add, change, or remove permissions as people change roles.

6) Incident Response and Digital Forensics
Brief:
Incident response and digital forensics is the emergency and investigation process used after a cyber incident. Incident response focuses on fast action: detect the issue, contain it, remove the threat, recover systems, and communicate updates to stakeholders. Digital forensics focuses on evidence: collecting logs and device data to understand exactly what happened, when it happened, and how attackers entered. It is used in companies, banks, schools, hospitals, and government organizations during ransomware attacks, account breaches, or data leaks. This practice reduces damage, supports legal and compliance reporting, and helps teams improve defenses to prevent repeat incidents.

7) Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC)
Brief:
Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) is the planning and policy side of cybersecurity that keeps security work aligned with business goals and legal requirements. Governance sets rules and responsibilities, risk management identifies what could cause harm and how serious it is, and compliance ensures required standards are met. It is used in all industries, especially finance, healthcare, e-commerce, and SaaS, where audits and regulations are common. GRC activities include writing policies, reviewing risks, tracking controls, and preparing audit evidence. Strong GRC helps organizations make smarter decisions, avoid penalties, and build trust with customers and regulators.

8) Security Awareness and Human Factors
Brief:
Security awareness and human factors focuses on how people behave when using technology, because human mistakes are one of the most common causes of security incidents. This area teaches practical habits such as spotting phishing emails, using strong passwords, reporting suspicious links, and handling sensitive data carefully. It is used in every workplace where staff use email, messaging apps, shared documents, and mobile devices. Organizations run awareness sessions, phishing simulations, and simple reporting procedures so employees know what to do under pressure. Over time, this creates a security-first culture where everyone helps prevent attacks before they cause harm.
